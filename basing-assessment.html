What This Is About

Priority

This started as a request to move Bravo Surgical Company back to Okinawa from Hawaii. That 2020 move created the problem we are fixing now. Over time the scope grew. What began as a single-company relocation turned into consolidating the entire 3d Medical Battalion at Camp Kinser. That means Headquarters and Service Company, Alpha and Bravo Surgical Companies, Motor Transport, Utilities, and the Medical Skills Training Center, all moving to one location.

The Commanding General signed the endorsement letter on 2 February 2026. The unit has already physically relocated. What we are doing now is the refresh: a walk and documentation pass to validate that the six designated buildings at Kinser actually support what the battalion needs, and to capture anything that still requires attention.

⚠

Why This Matters

Critical

When Bravo Surgical Company moved to Hawaii in 2020, the intent was to support Force Design initiatives. What actually happened is that the company got pulled into supporting Camp Smith, Tripler Army Medical Center, and Joint Base Pearl Harbor. Those are valid missions, but they are not III MEF missions. The surgical capacity that should be forward in the First Island Chain got diverted to Oahu.

In a rapid transition to conflict, every minute counts for trauma care. Having Role II surgical capability sitting in Hawaii instead of Okinawa means longer evacuation times, limited surgical capacity at the point of injury, and a direct hit to operational effectiveness. This consolidation fixes that by putting the full battalion where the fight is.

Command Intent

Get the entire medical battalion onto Camp Kinser. Colocate them with MEDLOG CLR-35 so deployable medical equipment is immediately accessible. Cut response times for rapid deployment. Keep the battalion intact as one unit instead of split across installations. Document the building conditions and any gaps so leadership knows exactly what we have and what still needs work.

Assessment Team Structure

The team working this refresh consists of one Planner and one Engineer, targeted to knock it out in a couple weeks. The Planner is already assigned and doing the read-in. The Engineer is still being identified from the available pool.

This two-person team structure is deliberate. It is lean enough to move fast but has the right skill mix: the Planner brings the basing process knowledge and documentation expertise, while the Engineer brings the technical building assessment and infrastructure eye. Together they can walk, measure, photograph, document, and produce the refreshed assessment without dragging in a larger working group.

Key point: Since the unit has already relocated, this refresh leans more toward a walk and documentation pass against the building list than a traditional feasibility study. The question is no longer "should we do this?" It is "what condition are these buildings in and do they actually fit what the battalion needs?"

The first sprint of work has a clear shape. Walk the buildings, net the requirements, account for the full battalion instead of just Bravo, and slot the results into the existing CG endorsement format.

Why Kinser Makes Sense

Proximity to MEDLOG

MEDLOG Company, CLR-35 is already on Camp Kinser. They house all the deployable medical equipment for 3d Med Bn. Putting the battalion on the same installation means equipment response time drops from cross-installation coordination to walking distance. In a rapid deployment scenario, that difference is measured in hours, not minutes.

Port Access

Camp Kinser sits near the port facilities in the western part of the island. For a medical battalion that may need to stage equipment for maritime prepositioning or force flow, that geographic advantage matters. Camp Foster and Camp Butler do not have the same immediate port proximity.

MLG Cohesion

Kinser is already a 3d MLG logistics hub. CLR-35, MEDLOG, and other support elements live there. Consolidating the medical battalion into that same hub strengthens the logistics group as a whole rather than fragmenting it across multiple installations.

Existing Infrastructure

Unlike a greenfield development at KASA-1 or Northern Training Area, Kinser already has buildings, roads, utilities, and communications. The unit is already there. The refresh is validating and documenting, not building from scratch. That drastically reduces activation timeline and cost.

Scorecard context from Tab B: When 3d MLG originally evaluated sites, Camp Foster scored 4.45, Camp Kinser scored 4.15, and Camp Hansen scored 3.20. The Kinser score was dragged down primarily on proximity to training areas (3 vs 5 for Foster). However, with the full battalion moving to Kinser and the MSTC co-located in Bldg. 300, that training gap is now internal to the footprint rather than requiring travel to external ranges.

Every basing action carries risk. The key risks here are armory capacity, DPRI alignment, and long-term sustainment funding. Understanding them upfront lets leadership make informed decisions about what to mitigate now versus what to accept as residual risk.

1

Armory Shortfall

Critical

The numbers do not lie. Even before adding Bravo, 3d Med Bn was utilizing 809 SF of armory against a 6,398 SF requirement. That is a 5,589 SF shortfall. Now add the full battalion. A dedicated bay in Bldg. 400 helps, but the consolidated armory is shared across multiple units. The realistic near-term fix is unit-procured ARMAG containers. The long-term fix requires either an armory expansion or a DPRI POR modification to retain the battalion in Okinawa. Neither is currently funded.

2

DPRI POR Misalignment

Strategic

The Defense Policy Review Initiative Okinawa Consolidation program does not plan for 100 percent of 3d MLG subordinate battalions to remain in Okinawa. Per the current POR, only task-organized detachments of Supply, Maintenance, and Medical battalions stay. The full battalion consolidation at Kinser is operationally sound but programmatically unsupported beyond roughly 2030. That creates a strategic risk: the unit may be asked to move again just as it gets settled.

★

Way Forward

Recommended

The near-term path is clear. Complete the walk, document the buildings, validate the space against the full battalion, produce the refreshed DOTMLPF-C, and slot it into the CG endorsement format. That closes the immediate documentation gap and gives MCICOM a clean package to endorse upward.

Beyond that, two strategic decisions sit with HQMC. First, whether to engage DPRI to modify the Program of Record to account for the full 3d Med Bn remaining in Okinawa. Second, whether to invest in new development areas (KASA-1, Central Training Area, or Northern Training Area) if enduring basing is required. Both options carry significant dollar and political cost. Neither is a base-level decision.

The practical middle ground is what Tab C recommended and what still holds: treat this as a short-term consolidation with unit-procured mitigation for immediate deficiencies, while the Service figures out the long-term posture. The unit gets the operational benefit of colocation now. The installation gets a documented baseline. HQMC gets a clean decision point.

Summary for Decision Makers

The unit is already at Kinser. The buildings are assigned. The operational benefit of colocation with MEDLOG is real and immediate. The assessment work validates and documents rather than debates feasibility. The risks are known: armory capacity, DPRI long-term alignment, and funding for any facility upgrades. The near-term fix is unit-procured equipment and a clean documentation pass. The long-term fix requires Service-level engagement on DPRI POR or new development investment. This is a sound operational decision with manageable near-term risk and a clear strategic fork ahead.
